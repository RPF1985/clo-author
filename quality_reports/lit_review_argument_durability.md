## Executive Summary

After extensive search across nine disciplinary literatures and 45+ papers reviewed in detail, the core finding of this literature review is:

**The proposed research question is genuinely novel, but the novelty claim must be carefully qualified against the belief perseverance literature.** No existing paper experimentally tests the structural durability of compound (hybrid) political arguments under partial disconfirmation. However, the belief perseverance tradition (Ross, Lepper & Hubbard 1975; Anderson, Lepper & Ross 1980) and the continued influence effect (Johnson & Seifert 1994) make a competing prediction: beliefs may PERSIST after partial disconfirmation, not collapse. The project's key contribution is pitting these competing predictions against each other in a new structural context.

The closest work exists in four literatures that have developed largely in isolation:

1. **Belief perseverance** (cognitive psychology): Shows that beliefs persist even after the evidential basis is completely discredited (Ross et al. 1975; Anderson et al. 1980). Predicts RESILIENCE of hybrid arguments.

2. **Argument quality/quantity** (social psychology): Shows that adding weak arguments to strong ones reduces initial persuasion via averaging (Obermaier & Koch 2024; Weaver et al. 2012). Predicts FRAGILITY of hybrid arguments.

3. **Framing and persuasion** (political science): Extensive work on how competing frames shape opinion (Chong & Druckman 2007, 2010, 2013). But studies COMPETING frames, not COMPLEMENTARY/STACKED ones.

4. **Misinformation correction** (cognitive/political psychology): Corrections generally work (Wood & Porter 2019), but continued influence persists when misinformation provided causal structure (Johnson & Seifert 1994; Lewandowsky et al. 2012). Constrains what the credibility cascade mechanism can claim.

**The specific novelty contributions are:**
- Concept of "argument structure durability" (emergent properties of justification combinations)
- First direct test of competing predictions: belief perseverance (resilience) vs. averaging/dilution (fragility) vs. rational updating (additivity) in compound political arguments
- "Credibility cascade" mechanism (disconfirmation of one justification undermines the surviving one), constrained by Wood & Porter (2019) to operate through rational inference
- Loss aversion applied to argument structure (losing a justification hurts more than gaining one helps)
- Cross-domain generality claim (tested across war, trade, climate)
- War domain grounded in just war theory (Walzer 1977; McMahan 2009) with empirical validation (Sagan & Valentino 2020)

**Scooping risk: LOW to MODERATE.** No working papers found targeting this question. The active argument quality/quantity literature (Bergman et al. 2025) and the booming misinformation correction literature are adjacent but distinct. Speed to pre-registration is advisable.

## Targeted Search Report: Third Pass (2026-04-03)

### Purpose

The paper has been reframed from a directional fragility hypothesis to "adjudicating between three theoretically derived competing predictions." This third-pass search specifically asks: **Has anyone already framed this as competing predictions and tested it?**

### Search Vector 1: Papers that pit perseverance against correction/updating

**Question:** Has anyone explicitly tested belief perseverance vs. rational updating for compound beliefs? Has anyone tested whether belief STRUCTURE (simple vs. compound) moderates the perseverance effect?

**Search terms used:** "belief perseverance" + "updating" + "compound" / "multiple reasons" / "structure"; Anderson et al. (1980) forward citations with structural moderators; "belief perseverance" + "meta-analysis" + "boundary conditions"

**Findings:**

1. **No paper was found that tests whether belief structure (simple vs. compound) moderates the perseverance effect.** The Anderson et al. (1980) forward citation chain includes Anderson & Kellam (1992) on covariation detection and Anderson's own chapter on self-defeating behavior (1989), but no study manipulates the NUMBER or TYPE of supporting reasons as a moderator of perseverance.

2. **Wanke (2010)** tested the influence of accessibility experiences on belief perseverance when participants generated few or many reasons. Key finding: participants who listed MANY reasons about a reported outcome showed NO perseverance (the difficulty of generating many reasons undermined belief), while those who listed MANY reasons for an ALTERNATIVE showed MORE perseverance. This is about the subjective experience of generating reasons (ease/difficulty), not about whether having multiple pre-existing reasons makes a belief more or less resilient. Tangentially relevant (proximity 2) but does not test structural complexity.

3. **Little (2024)** in Political Behavior distinguishes "once-motivated reasoning" (wanting to believe) from "twice-motivated reasoning" (rejecting signals) and shows that once-motivated reasoning is observationally equivalent to Bayesian updating with different priors. This is methodologically relevant for the argument-durability project's rational updating prediction (H1c) but does not test compound belief structures.

**Bottom line for Vector 1:** The specific question of whether belief STRUCTURE moderates perseverance is untested. This is a genuine gap.

### Search Vector 2: Papers testing resilience of multi-attribute attitudes

**Question:** In consumer behavior, what happens to product attitudes when one attribute claim fails but others remain? When an attitude has dual bases (cognitive + affective), does removing one make the other stronger or weaker?

**Search terms used:** "multi-attribute" + "disconfirmation" + "resilience"; "dual basis" + "attitude" + "attack"; "product recall" + "brand attitude" + "spillover"; Fabrigar & Petty (1999) matching effects

**Findings:**

1. **No paper was found that tests what happens to a multi-attribute evaluation when one specific attribute is disconfirmed while others remain.** The multi-attribute attitude literature (Fishbein) focuses on how attributes combine to form overall evaluations, not on what happens when one attribute's claim is falsified post-formation.

2. **Fabrigar & Petty (1999)** showed that persuasive appeals matching the attitude's basis (affective or cognitive) are more effective than mismatched appeals. This establishes that attitudes have structural bases that matter for susceptibility to change, but does not test what happens when one basis is REMOVED or disconfirmed.

3. **Product recall/spillover literature** examines whether a recall on one product contaminates attitudes toward OTHER products of the same brand or competing brands. This is a between-product spillover question, not a within-attitude structural question. The closest analog is Ahluwalia et al. (2000) on negative publicity, already in the bibliography.

4. **The Dalege et al. (2016) Causal Attitude Network (CAN) model** conceptualizes attitudes as networks of interconnected evaluative reactions (beliefs, feelings, behaviors). It predicts that highly connected networks resist change (strong attitudes) and that central nodes have larger influence when changed. This provides THEORETICAL scaffolding for the argument-durability project: the model predicts that what happens when one node is disconfirmed depends on how connected it is to other nodes. However, the CAN model has not been tested with the specific paradigm of disconfirming one justification type while another survives.

5. **Brandt & Sleegers (2021)** implemented the belief system network approach as a dynamic Ising model, showing that changes in one belief can "spill over" to connected beliefs. They demonstrated that the network framework can account for six otherwise unrelated phenomena in political belief system dynamics. This is relevant as theoretical infrastructure but does not test the specific competing predictions about compound argument durability.

**Bottom line for Vector 2:** No direct test exists. The attitude network literature provides the closest theoretical framework but has not been applied to the specific question of compound justification durability under partial disconfirmation.

### Search Vector 3: Meta-analyses of perseverance effects and boundary conditions

**Question:** Are there meta-analyses of belief perseverance? What boundary conditions have been identified? Does anyone identify "number of supporting reasons" or "structural complexity" as a moderator?

**Search terms used:** "belief perseverance" + "meta-analysis" + "boundary conditions" + "moderators"; Chan et al. 2017 debunking meta-analysis

**Findings:**

1. **Chan et al. (2017)** conducted the most relevant meta-analysis (k = 52, N = 6,878) on debunking misinformation. They found large effects for misinformation presentation (d = 2.41-3.08), debunking (d = 1.14-1.33), and misinformation persistence despite debunking (d = 0.75-1.06). The moderators they identified were: (a) whether audiences generated reasons supporting the misinformation (increases persistence), (b) whether audiences generated counterarguments (decreases persistence), and (c) detail level of the debunking message. **Critically, they did NOT test whether the number of reasons supporting a belief, or the structural complexity of the belief, moderates debunking effectiveness.** This is an untested moderator in the meta-analytic literature.

2. **The Oxford Bibliographies entry on belief perseverance** lists known moderators including: dogmatism (high dogmatism increases perseverance), explanation generation (amplifies perseverance per Anderson et al. 1980), consideration of alternatives (reduces perseverance), and evidence quality. "Number of supporting reasons" or "structural complexity" is not listed as a tested moderator.

3. **No dedicated meta-analysis of belief perseverance per se** was found (as distinct from the debunking/misinformation correction meta-analyses). The Chan et al. (2017) and subsequent correction meta-analyses focus on misinformation contexts specifically.

**Bottom line for Vector 3:** "Structural complexity" and "number of supporting reasons" are untested as moderators of perseverance in the meta-analytic literature. This is a clear gap that the argument-durability project would fill.

### Search Vector 4: Competing predictions framing in adjacent work

**Question:** Has anyone in political science or psychology framed a study as "perseverance vs. correction for political attitudes"? Has anyone tested whether compound justifications for policy positions behave differently than single justifications under challenge?

**Search terms used:** "competing predictions" + "perseverance" + "correction" + political attitudes; Chong & Druckman + "reinforcing frames" / "complementary frames"; "fragility" OR "resilience" + "compound" / "hybrid" + "political argument" experiment 2020-2025

**Findings:**

1. **No paper was found that frames a study as testing perseverance vs. averaging/dilution vs. rational updating for compound political beliefs.** This three-way framing appears to be genuinely novel.

2. **Druckman (2023, PNAS)** tested whether correcting misperceptions of partisan opponents reduces support for undemocratic practices. Found that basic corrections work in isolation but not in competitive information environments. This is about correction effectiveness in context, not about structural properties of compound beliefs.

3. **Chong & Druckman's framing program** has not addressed "reinforcing" or "complementary" frames. Their work focuses on COMPETING frames (opposing arguments) and how frame strength, timing, and repetition affect which frame dominates. The specific search for "reinforcing frames," "complementary frames," and "stacked frames" in their citation network returned no results.

4. **No SSRN working paper, EGAP registration, or institutional working paper** was found that combines "hybrid/compound argument" with "durability/disconfirmation" in a political context (searched 2023-2026).

**Bottom line for Vector 4:** The competing predictions framing is novel. No one has set up the perseverance-vs.-averaging-vs.-updating test for compound political arguments.

### Search Vector 5: Loss aversion applied to beliefs or arguments

**Question:** Has anyone tested whether losing a reason hurts more than gaining one helps, in the context of political attitudes?

**Search terms used:** "loss aversion" + "belief" / "argument" / "reason" / "justification"; "gain loss asymmetry" + persuasion + "adding argument" versus "removing argument"

**Findings:**

1. **Alesina & Passarelli (2019, AJPS)** is the most prominent application of loss aversion to politics. They model how loss-averse voters prefer the status quo and show that loss aversion yields moderation, status quo bias, and path dependence. This is about POLICY loss aversion (fear of changing policy status quo), not about ARGUMENT loss aversion (losing a justification for one's position). Different domain of application.

2. **Osmundsen & Petersen (2020, Political Psychology)** showed that loss aversion operates differently in personal vs. political settings: high-resource individuals are MORE loss-averse in political (group) settings, reversing the personal-setting pattern. Again, this is about risk framing and policy gambles, not about gaining/losing justifications.

3. **No paper was found that applies loss aversion specifically to the gain vs. loss of justifications within compound arguments.** The Aronson & Linder (1965) gain-loss theory in interpersonal attraction is the closest analog (esteem gains feel different from esteem losses), but this has not been extended to justification structure.

4. **Baumeister et al. (2001) "Bad Is Stronger Than Good"** (already in bibliography) provides the broadest negativity bias framework that would predict the loss > gain asymmetry in argument structure, but does not test it in this specific context.

**Bottom line for Vector 5:** Loss aversion has been applied to policy preferences (Alesina & Passarelli) and risk framing (Osmundsen & Petersen) but NOT to the structural gain/loss of justifications within compound arguments. H2 (asymmetric gain/loss in argument structure) is novel.

### Overall Novelty Verdict (Third Pass)

**CONFIRMED: The three-way competing predictions framing is novel.** After targeted search across five specific vectors:

1. No paper pits perseverance vs. averaging/dilution vs. rational updating for compound political beliefs.
2. No paper tests whether belief structural complexity moderates the perseverance effect.
3. No meta-analysis identifies "number of supporting reasons" as a moderator of perseverance or correction effectiveness.
4. No paper in the Chong & Druckman or Nyhan & Reifler citation chains addresses reinforcing (complementary) frames under disconfirmation.
5. No paper applies loss aversion to the gain/loss of justifications within compound arguments.

The closest work that could potentially overlap:
- **Dalege et al. (2016) CAN model** provides theoretical infrastructure (attitudes as networks of connected evaluative reactions) that could generate similar predictions, but has not been tested with compound justification paradigms.
- **Brandt & Sleegers (2021)** show belief change can propagate through networks, which maps onto the "credibility cascade" mechanism, but in a much more general framework without the specific competing predictions.
- **Wanke (2010)** shows that the EXPERIENCE of generating reasons (ease/difficulty) moderates perseverance, but not the structural fact of having multiple reasons.

**The novelty assessment from the second pass stands. If anything, the third pass STRENGTHENS the novelty claim** by confirming that the specific structural question (does compound vs. simple justificatory structure moderate the response to partial disconfirmation?) has not been asked in any of the adjacent literatures where one might expect to find it.

**Scooping risk remains LOW to MODERATE.** No new threats detected.
