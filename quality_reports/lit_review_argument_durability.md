## Executive Summary

After extensive search across nine disciplinary literatures and 45+ papers reviewed in detail, the core finding of this literature review is:

**The proposed research question is genuinely novel, but the novelty claim must be carefully qualified against the belief perseverance literature.** No existing paper experimentally tests the structural durability of compound (hybrid) political arguments under partial disconfirmation. However, the belief perseverance tradition (Ross, Lepper & Hubbard 1975; Anderson, Lepper & Ross 1980) and the continued influence effect (Johnson & Seifert 1994) make a competing prediction: beliefs may PERSIST after partial disconfirmation, not collapse. The project's key contribution is pitting these competing predictions against each other in a new structural context.

The closest work exists in four literatures that have developed largely in isolation:

1. **Belief perseverance** (cognitive psychology): Shows that beliefs persist even after the evidential basis is completely discredited (Ross et al. 1975; Anderson et al. 1980). Predicts RESILIENCE of hybrid arguments.

2. **Argument quality/quantity** (social psychology): Shows that adding weak arguments to strong ones reduces initial persuasion via averaging (Obermaier & Koch 2024; Weaver et al. 2012). Predicts FRAGILITY of hybrid arguments.

3. **Framing and persuasion** (political science): Extensive work on how competing frames shape opinion (Chong & Druckman 2007, 2010, 2013). But studies COMPETING frames, not COMPLEMENTARY/STACKED ones.

4. **Misinformation correction** (cognitive/political psychology): Corrections generally work (Wood & Porter 2019), but continued influence persists when misinformation provided causal structure (Johnson & Seifert 1994; Lewandowsky et al. 2012). Constrains what the credibility cascade mechanism can claim.

**The specific novelty contributions are:**
- Concept of "argument structure durability" (emergent properties of justification combinations)
- First direct test of competing predictions: belief perseverance (resilience) vs. averaging/dilution (fragility) in compound political arguments
- "Credibility cascade" mechanism (disconfirmation of one justification undermines the surviving one), constrained by Wood & Porter (2019) to operate through rational inference
- Loss aversion applied to argument structure (losing a justification hurts more than gaining one helps)
- Cross-domain generality claim (tested across war, trade, climate)
- War domain grounded in just war theory (Walzer 1977; McMahan 2009) with empirical validation (Sagan & Valentino 2020)

**Scooping risk: LOW to MODERATE.** No working papers found targeting this question. The active argument quality/quantity literature (Bergman et al. 2025) and the booming misinformation correction literature are adjacent but distinct. Speed to pre-registration is advisable.
