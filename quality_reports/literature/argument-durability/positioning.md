# Positioning Statement: Argument Durability Under Partial Disconfirmation

**Date:** 2026-04-03 (Revised: 2026-04-03, second pass)

---

## Novelty Assessment (Revised)

### Has anyone made this specific argument before?

**No -- but the claim must be carefully qualified against the belief perseverance literature.**

After extensive cross-disciplinary search (political science, social psychology, communication studies, moral psychology, behavioral economics, consumer behavior, philosophy, misinformation/correction research, just war theory), no paper was found that:

1. Directly tests the durability of hybrid (normative + practical) political arguments under partial disconfirmation
2. Proposes or tests a "credibility cascade" mechanism from one disconfirmed justification to a surviving one
3. Applies loss aversion to the gain/loss of individual justifications within compound arguments
4. Compares hybrid vs. pure argument durability experimentally

### The Belief Perseverance Challenge

**The belief perseverance literature (Ross, Lepper & Hubbard 1975; Anderson, Lepper & Ross 1980) represents the strongest theoretical challenge to the fragility hypothesis.** Its core finding is that beliefs PERSIST even after the evidential basis is completely discredited. If this applies to hybrid arguments, the prediction would be RESILIENCE, not fragility.

**Key papers and their implications:**

1. **Ross, Lepper & Hubbard (1975):** Subjects told their performance feedback was fabricated continued to believe it. This challenges the fragility hypothesis by suggesting that once an impression forms, removing its basis does not remove the impression.

2. **Anderson, Lepper & Ross (1980):** Once people generate causal explanations connecting evidence to a theory, discrediting the evidence does not eliminate the theory. Explanation generation creates independent cognitive support structures. This DIRECTLY predicts that hybrid arguments should be MORE durable: people with two justifications have generated richer explanatory structures that survive partial disconfirmation.

3. **Johnson & Seifert (1994):** Discredited information continues to influence reasoning when it provides causal structure. Providing an alternative explanation mitigates the continued influence effect. In hybrid arguments, the surviving justification IS the alternative explanation -- which the CIE literature predicts should FACILITATE clean updating, not cascade failure.

4. **Wood & Porter (2019):** Across 52 issues and 10,100+ subjects, backfire effects are essentially non-existent. People generally accept factual corrections. This CONSTRAINS what the credibility cascade can claim: the cascade cannot operate through motivated irrationality or reactance.

### How the Argument-Durability Project Differs from Belief Perseverance

The proposed project is NOT simply a replication of belief perseverance with compound arguments. The distinction rests on three differences:

| Dimension | Belief Perseverance Studies | This Project |
|---|---|---|
| **Belief structure** | Single evidential basis (one piece of feedback, one data set) | Compound justificatory structure (normative + practical) |
| **Type of discrediting** | "The evidence was fabricated" (total undermining of a single basis) | "This specific justification turns out to be false" (partial undermining of compound structure) |
| **Availability of alternative** | No alternative explanation provided (in classic paradigm) | Alternative justification is ALREADY PRESENT (the surviving leg) |
| **Context** | Abstract or novel topics (firefighter performance, anagram tasks) | Politically charged topics where motivated reasoning is active |

**The critical theoretical question:** In belief perseverance, the ABSENCE of an alternative explanation is what drives persistence (people cling to the discredited belief because they have nothing else). In hybrid arguments, the PRESENCE of a surviving alternative may change the dynamic in either direction:

- **Fragility prediction:** The surviving justification's quality is revealed as insufficient when viewed alone -- the person realizes "I was relying on both reasons, and one was false, so maybe my overall judgment was wrong." The disconfirmation triggers re-evaluation of the ENTIRE position, not just the disconfirmed component. This is the "credibility cascade."

- **Resilience prediction (belief perseverance):** The surviving justification provides a ready-made alternative that allows clean updating. People say "OK, the practical reason was wrong, but the moral reason still stands" and maintain their position. The Anderson et al. (1980) explanation-generation mechanism predicts this: people who articulated BOTH justifications have built richer cognitive structures that survive partial pruning.

**The proposed experiment can discriminate between these predictions.** The hybrid-vs-pure comparison is the test: if hybrid arguments show GREATER support decline than pure arguments after matched disconfirmation (fragility wins), or SMALLER decline (resilience/perseverance wins). This is what makes the project novel -- it pits two well-established psychological mechanisms against each other in a new structural context.

### What Is Closest?

**Tier 1: Most directly relevant (proximity 4-5)**

1. **Anderson, Lepper & Ross (1980)** -- The strongest theoretical competitor. Predicts resilience through explanation-generation. But studied single beliefs with fabricated evidence, not compound political arguments with real-world disconfirmation.

2. **Obermaier & Koch (2024)** -- Shows that adding weak arguments undermines strong arguments through averaging. Closest conceptual neighbor on the "fragility" side but studies INITIAL persuasion, not post-formation durability.

3. **Weaver, Garcia & Schwarz (2012)** -- The Presenter's Paradox demonstrates averaging in evaluation. Same limitation: about initial bundling, not about what happens when a bundle component fails.

4. **Johnson & Seifert (1994)** -- The continued influence effect shows discredited info persists when it provides causal structure. But studies single-item misinformation, not compound justificatory structures.

5. **Wood & Porter (2019)** -- Constrains the credibility cascade mechanism by showing people DO update on corrections. The cascade must work through rational inference, not irrational reactance.

**Tier 2: Related but distinct (proximity 3)**

6. **Chong & Druckman (2007, 2010, 2013)** -- Comprehensive program on competing frames. Key distinction: their work is about OPPOSING frames; this project is about REINFORCING frames.

7. **Shafir, Simonson & Tversky (1993)** -- Reason-based choice predicts that people who articulate reasons are MORE disrupted when a reason is removed, because the removal creates a specific gap in justificatory structure. This supports the fragility prediction.

8. **Fabrigar & Petty (1999)** -- Matched attacks on attitude bases. Extends to asking: what happens when an attitude has DUAL bases and one is attacked?

### How Does This Paper Differ?

| Dimension | Existing Literature | This Paper |
|---|---|---|
| **Timing** | Studies argument effects at formation (averaging lit) or persistence of single beliefs (perseverance lit) | Studies compound argument effects under post-formation disconfirmation |
| **Frame relationship** | Competing (opposed) frames | Complementary (stacked/reinforcing) frames |
| **Argument manipulation** | Adding arguments (averaging) or discrediting single beliefs (perseverance) | Selectively disconfirming one component of a compound structure |
| **Mechanism** | Averaging, dilution, explanation-generation, continued influence | Credibility cascade (new): disconfirmation of one undermines the other |
| **Domain** | Usually single domain | Cross-domain generality claim |
| **Loss aversion** | Applied to money, possessions, beliefs-as-objects | Applied to justifications-within-arguments |
| **Competing predictions** | Each literature makes a one-directional prediction | Design explicitly tests fragility vs. resilience |

## Suggested Contribution Statement (Revised)

**For the introduction/abstract:**

> We introduce the concept of argument structure durability -- the idea that the combination of justifications supporting a political position has emergent properties that differ from the individual justifications considered separately. A large literature on belief perseverance shows that beliefs formed from subsequently discredited evidence can persist (Ross, Lepper & Hubbard 1975; Anderson, Lepper & Ross 1980), while a parallel literature on argument quality shows that adding weaker arguments dilutes stronger ones (Obermaier & Koch 2024; Weaver, Garcia & Schwarz 2012). These literatures make opposing predictions about what happens when one component of a compound political justification is disconfirmed. We provide the first experimental test of this question: are hybrid arguments -- positions supported by both normative and practical justifications -- more fragile or more resilient than pure arguments when partially disconfirmed? We find that [results], that this effect operates through [mechanism], and that losing a justification reduces support more than gaining one increases it.

**For the literature review positioning paragraph:**

> Our study connects four literatures that have developed largely in isolation. First, the belief perseverance tradition (Ross et al. 1975; Anderson et al. 1980; Johnson & Seifert 1994) demonstrates that beliefs can survive the complete discrediting of their evidential basis, predicting that hybrid arguments should be resilient. Second, the argument quality and quantity tradition (Petty & Cacioppo 1984; Obermaier & Koch 2024; Weaver et al. 2012) shows that adding weak arguments can dilute strong ones through averaging, predicting that hybrid arguments should be fragile. Third, the framing and persuasion literature in political science has extensively studied competing frames (Chong & Druckman 2007; Druckman 2022) but has not examined reinforcing frames under stress. Fourth, the misinformation correction literature (Nyhan & Reifler 2010; Wood & Porter 2019; Lewandowsky et al. 2012) shows that corrections generally work but that continued influence effects persist when discredited information provides causal structure. We bridge these literatures by asking: when a political position rests on both normative and practical justifications, does the compound structure create fragility or resilience when one justification is disconfirmed?

## Differentiation from Specific Papers (for referee responses)

### "How is this different from belief perseverance (Ross et al. 1975; Anderson et al. 1980)?"
Belief perseverance studies examine what happens when the ENTIRE evidential basis for a SINGLE belief is discredited. We study what happens when ONE of TWO justifications for a political position is disconfirmed while the other remains intact. The key structural difference is the presence of a surviving alternative justification. In classic perseverance studies, there is no alternative -- people persist because they have nothing else. In our design, people DO have something else (the surviving justification), and the question is whether that surviving justification is strengthened (compensatory resilience), unaffected (independence), or weakened (credibility cascade) by the partner's failure.

### "How is this different from the continued influence effect (Johnson & Seifert 1994)?"
The CIE shows that discredited information continues to influence reasoning, especially when it provided causal structure. Our design asks a different question: does discrediting one justification undermine ANOTHER justification that was never itself discredited? The CIE is about persistence of the discredited item; the credibility cascade is about contamination of the non-discredited item.

### "Doesn't Wood & Porter (2019) rule out the credibility cascade?"
No. Wood & Porter show that people generally accept factual corrections -- they do not "backfire" into stronger belief. Our credibility cascade does not require backfire. It requires only that disconfirmation of one justification reduces confidence in the OTHER justification beyond what its independent standing would predict. This can operate through rational inference ("if they were wrong about this, maybe the other claim is also suspect") rather than motivated irrationality. Wood & Porter's finding that corrections work actually SUPPORTS our design: it predicts that the disconfirmation manipulation WILL move attitudes, making the key question HOW MUCH it moves them in hybrid vs. pure conditions.

### "How is this different from the Presenter's Paradox (Weaver et al. 2012)?"
The Presenter's Paradox shows that ADDING mildly positive items REDUCES evaluations through averaging. We study what happens when an already-formed compound evaluation is DISRUPTED by the active disconfirmation of one component. This is a different manipulation (removal vs. addition), a different stage (post-formation vs. formation), and a different mechanism (credibility cascade vs. averaging).

### "How is this different from Chong & Druckman's framing work?"
Chong & Druckman study COMPETING frames -- opposing arguments for different positions. We study COMPLEMENTARY frames -- reinforcing arguments for the SAME position. Their question is: which frame wins? Our question is: what happens to a position when one of its supporting legs fails?

### "How is this different from Taber & Lodge (2006) on motivated reasoning?"
Taber & Lodge show that people resist disconfirming information about their beliefs. We ask a structural question: does having multiple justificatory bases CHANGE how people process disconfirmation? Motivated reasoning predicts resistance in all conditions; we test whether the STRUCTURE of justification moderates that resistance.

### "How is this different from the dilution effect (Nisbett et al. 1981)?"
The dilution effect involves adding NONDIAGNOSTIC (irrelevant) information that weakens diagnostic information. Our disconfirmation involves a previously DIAGNOSTIC piece of information being actively falsified. This is stronger than dilution -- it is not merely adding noise but actively removing a pillar.

### "How is this different from Obermaier & Koch (2024) on argument strength?"
Obermaier & Koch show that weak arguments dilute strong arguments at the point of initial persuasion. We test what happens AFTER initial persuasion, when one argument is disconfirmed. Their finding predicts our H1 (hybrid arguments start weaker), but our contribution is showing the dynamic consequences: the fragility/resilience of the hybrid structure under stress, the credibility cascade mechanism, and the loss > gain asymmetry.

### "How does the just war framework inform the war domain scenarios?"
The normative/practical distinction in our experimental design maps directly onto just war theory's jus ad bellum categories (Walzer 1977; McMahan 2009). "Just cause" and moral obligation are normative justifications; "reasonable chance of success" and proportionality are consequentialist/practical ones. Sagan & Valentino (2020) show that the American public applies moral reasoning to war evaluation in ways that track these categories. Our war scenarios thus have construct validity grounded in a well-established philosophical and empirical tradition.
