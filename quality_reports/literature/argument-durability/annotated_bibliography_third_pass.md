## Category 8: THIRD-PASS ADDITIONS -- Belief Perseverance Moderators

### Wanke (2010) -- "Belief Perseverance: The Role of Accessible Content and Accessibility Experiences"
- **Journal:** Social Psychology, 41(1), 26-34
- **Summary:** Two studies tested the influence of accessibility experiences on belief perseverance. Participants generated few or many reasons about how a reported outcome or an alternative outcome might have occurred. Key finding: participants who listed MANY reasons about the reported outcome showed NO perseverance (the subjective difficulty of generating many reasons undermined belief), while those who listed MANY reasons for an ALTERNATIVE showed MORE perseverance. This demonstrates that the EXPERIENCE of generating reasons (ease vs. difficulty) matters more than the content of the reasons themselves.
- **Identification strategy:** Between-subjects experiment manipulating number of reasons generated.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments with undergraduates.
- **Main result:** Accessibility experiences (ease/difficulty of generating reasons) moderate belief perseverance; generating many reasons can paradoxically reduce perseverance.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Tangentially relevant. Tests whether generating reasons moderates perseverance, but through the accessibility-experience mechanism (ease/difficulty), not through the structural fact of having multiple pre-existing reasons. The argument-durability project asks a different question: does having TWO TYPES of justification (normative + practical) create a structure that is more or less resilient, regardless of how easy they were to generate. Wanke's finding is about meta-cognitive fluency, not about justificatory structure.

### Chan, Jones, Hall Jamieson & Albarracin (2017) -- "Debunking: A Meta-Analysis of the Psychological Efficacy of Messages Countering Misinformation"
- **Journal:** Psychological Science, 28(11), 1531-1546
- **Summary:** Meta-analysis of 52 studies (N = 6,878) on debunking misinformation. Large effects for misinformation presentation (d = 2.41-3.08), debunking (d = 1.14-1.33), and misinformation persistence despite debunking (d = 0.75-1.06). Moderators identified: (a) audience generation of reasons supporting misinformation increases persistence, (b) audience counterarguing decreases persistence, (c) detailed debunking messages improve correction but paradoxically also increase persistence.
- **Identification strategy:** Random-effects meta-analysis with moderator analysis.
- **Key data source:** 52 published studies on misinformation correction.
- **Main result:** Debunking reduces but does not eliminate misinformation effects. Explanation generation and message detail are key moderators.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Important for the argument-durability project because it identifies moderators of correction effectiveness. Critically, this meta-analysis does NOT test "number of supporting reasons" or "structural complexity of the belief" as a moderator. This is a confirmed gap: the argument-durability project would be the first to test whether the structure of the belief (compound vs. simple) moderates how effectively disconfirmation works.

## Category 9: THIRD-PASS ADDITIONS -- Attitude Network Models

### Dalege, Borsboom, van Harreveld, van den Berg, Conner & van der Maas (2016) -- "Toward a Formalized Account of Attitudes: The Causal Attitude Network (CAN) Model"
- **Journal:** Psychological Review, 123(1), 2-22
- **Summary:** Proposes the Causal Attitude Network model, which conceptualizes attitudes as networks of causally connected evaluative reactions (beliefs, feelings, behaviors). Central postulate: highly connected attitude networks correspond to strong attitudes. Simulations show that (a) highly connected networks are more resistant to change, (b) central nodes have outsized influence when changed, and (c) highly connected networks produce bimodal (polarized) attitude distributions. The model integrates attitude strength, ambivalence, and polarization within a single framework.
- **Identification strategy:** Formal computational model with Ising model simulations.
- **Key data source:** Simulated data; validated against published attitude research.
- **Main result:** Attitudes as networks of connected evaluative reactions; connectivity predicts strength, resistance, and polarization.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Provides the most sophisticated theoretical framework for understanding the argument-durability question. In CAN terms, the question becomes: when a node (justification) in the attitude network is set to negative (disconfirmed), does the disruption propagate through the network (fragility) or is the network resilient because the remaining node holds the overall evaluation stable? The CAN model predicts that the answer depends on network connectivity: highly connected attitudes should be more resistant to single-node disruption. However, the CAN model has been tested primarily with correlational data and simulations, not with the specific paradigm of disconfirming one justification in a compound political argument. The argument-durability project provides a direct behavioral test of a CAN-adjacent prediction.

### Brandt & Sleegers (2021) -- "Evaluating Belief System Networks as a Theory of Political Belief System Dynamics"
- **Journal:** Personality and Social Psychology Review, 25(2), 159-185
- **Summary:** Implements the belief system network approach as a dynamic Ising model to explain six otherwise unrelated phenomena in the political belief systems literature: attitude consistency, cross-pressures, spillover effects of attitude change, partisan cue effects, ideological differences in attitude consensus, and polarization. Shows that a change in one belief element can propagate through the network and influence connected beliefs ("spillover").
- **Identification strategy:** Computational modeling with Ising model simulations.
- **Key data source:** Simulated data validated against published political psychology findings.
- **Main result:** Belief system network theory can integrate six distinct phenomena; attitude change propagates through connected belief networks.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- The "spillover" prediction is directly relevant to the credibility cascade mechanism: if normative and practical justifications are connected in a belief network, disconfirmation of one should propagate to the other. Brandt & Sleegers show this is theoretically possible but do not test the specific case of compound justifications under partial disconfirmation. The argument-durability project provides the first behavioral data on this specific structural question.

## Category 10: THIRD-PASS ADDITIONS -- Motivated Reasoning vs. Bayesian Updating

### Little (2024) -- "How to Distinguish Motivated Reasoning from Bayesian Updating"
- **Journal:** Political Behavior (published online 2024)
- **Summary:** Formalizes the distinction between motivated reasoning and Bayesian updating. Shows that "once-motivated reasoning" (wanting to believe certain things) is observationally equivalent to Bayesian updating with different priors -- they cannot be distinguished by observing information responses. Introduces "twice-motivated reasoning" (sometimes completely rejecting inconvenient signals) which CAN be distinguished from Bayesian updating because it generates predictions without a Bayesian equivalent.
- **Identification strategy:** Formal theoretical model with behavioral implications.
- **Key data source:** Theoretical; simulated.
- **Main result:** Once-motivated reasoning has a Bayesian equivalent; twice-motivated reasoning does not. The two can be distinguished by testing whether beliefs respond to signals in a direction-dependent manner.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Methodologically relevant for the rational updating prediction (H1c). If the argument-durability project's "additivity" prediction corresponds to Bayesian updating, Little's framework clarifies what that means operationally: support declines proportionally to the Bayesian weight of the disconfirmed justification, with no direction-dependent distortion. The framework also helps interpret the resilience prediction: if hybrid arguments show less decline than expected, this could reflect either twice-motivated reasoning (rejecting the disconfirmation) or genuine structural resilience. The experimental design (with manipulation checks and credibility measures) can help distinguish these.

## Category 11: THIRD-PASS ADDITIONS -- Loss Aversion in Politics

### Alesina & Passarelli (2019) -- "Loss Aversion in Politics"
- **Journal:** American Journal of Political Science, 63(4), 936-947
- **Summary:** Models how loss-averse voters affect political outcomes. Shows that loss aversion produces: (a) moderation of policy proposals, (b) status quo bias with path dependence, (c) diminished loss aversion effects over longer planning horizons, and (d) in stochastic environments, distaste for risk but smaller attachment to status quo. The model is about voters' aversion to POLICY CHANGE, not about argument structure.
- **Identification strategy:** Formal theoretical model of majority voting.
- **Key data source:** Theoretical.
- **Main result:** Loss aversion in politics produces moderation, status quo bias, and path dependence.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- The most prominent application of loss aversion to politics, but in a fundamentally different domain. Alesina & Passarelli model aversion to changing policy from the status quo. The argument-durability project applies loss aversion to the gain vs. loss of JUSTIFICATIONS within compound arguments (H2). These are different objects of loss aversion: policy positions vs. argumentative support. The distinction matters: the argument-durability project is not claiming that people are loss-averse about policy change but that losing a justification reduces support more than gaining one increases it.

### Osmundsen & Petersen (2020) -- "Framing Political Risks: Individual Differences and Loss Aversion in Personal and Political Situations"
- **Journal:** Political Psychology, 41(1), 53-70
- **Summary:** Tests whether loss aversion operates identically in personal and political risk settings. Key finding: loss aversion is moderated by individual differences in resource capacity, but the direction REVERSES between personal and political contexts. In personal settings, low-resource individuals are more loss-averse (consistent with standard theory). In political settings (where group resources are threatened), HIGH-resource individuals are more loss-averse. This suggests loss aversion has different psychological substrates in personal vs. political domains.
- **Identification strategy:** Survey experiments manipulating gain/loss framing in personal and political scenarios.
- **Key data source:** Survey experiments with representative samples.
- **Main result:** Loss aversion in political settings is moderated differently than in personal settings; direction reverses based on individual resource capacity.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Relevant for H2 (gain/loss asymmetry in argument structure) because it demonstrates that loss aversion operates in political attitudes but with different dynamics than in personal decisions. If the argument-durability project finds that losing a justification hurts more than gaining one helps, Osmundsen & Petersen's work suggests that individual differences (political engagement, expertise) may moderate this asymmetry in political-specific ways.

## Updated Gap Summary (Third Pass)

In addition to the six gaps identified in the second pass, the third-pass targeted search confirmed:

7. **No paper tests whether belief structural complexity (number or type of supporting reasons) moderates the perseverance effect.** The Anderson et al. (1980) forward citation chain and the Chan et al. (2017) meta-analysis both fail to identify this as a tested moderator.

8. **No paper applies the attitude network framework (Dalege et al. 2016 CAN model) to the specific case of compound justification durability under partial disconfirmation.** The CAN model provides the theoretical tools, but the behavioral test has not been conducted.

9. **No paper frames a study as "perseverance vs. averaging/dilution vs. rational updating" for compound political beliefs.** This three-way test appears to be genuinely novel.

10. **No paper applies loss aversion to the gain/loss of justifications within compound arguments.** Alesina & Passarelli (2019) apply it to policy preferences; Osmundsen & Petersen (2020) to risk framing. Neither addresses argument structure.
