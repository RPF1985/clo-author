# Frontier Map: Argument Durability Under Partial Disconfirmation

**Date:** 2026-04-03 (Revised: 2026-04-03, second pass)

---

## What Has Been Done

### 1. Belief Perseverance and Continued Influence (well-developed)

**Added in second pass -- this is the most important competing tradition.**

The belief perseverance literature demonstrates that beliefs can survive the complete discrediting of their evidential basis:
- Once beliefs form, standard debriefing does not eliminate them (Ross, Lepper & Hubbard 1975)
- Generating causal explanations creates independent cognitive supports that survive evidence removal; perseverance amplified by ~20-30% when explanations are generated (Anderson, Lepper & Ross 1980)
- People evaluate new evidence through the lens of prior beliefs; mixed evidence produces polarization, not moderation (Lord, Ross & Lepper 1979)
- Discredited misinformation continues to influence reasoning when it provided causal structure; providing an alternative explanation mitigates this (Johnson & Seifert 1994)
- Comprehensive review confirms that retractions rarely eliminate reliance on misinformation fully; alternative explanations are needed (Lewandowsky et al. 2012)

**Gap:** This literature studies beliefs with SINGLE evidential bases. No perseverance study has tested what happens when a belief rests on COMPOUND justificatory structure (two independent reasons of different types) and one is selectively disconfirmed while the other remains intact. The presence of a surviving justification changes the dynamic: it serves as the "alternative explanation" that the CIE literature shows can mitigate continued influence, but it may also reveal the insufficiency of the remaining support.

### 2. Backfire Effects and Corrections (contested, resolved toward "no backfire")
- Original backfire effect documented but not robustly replicable (Nyhan & Reifler 2010)
- Large-scale tests across 52 issues find NO backfire; people generally accept corrections (Wood & Porter 2019)
- The "elusive backfire" finding constrains what the credibility cascade mechanism can claim

**Gap:** The correction literature studies correction of factual claims, not removal of justificatory components from compound arguments. The credibility cascade mechanism must work through rational inference, not irrational reactance.

### 3. Framing Effects and Competing Frames (well-developed)
The literature on how alternative frames shift opinion is mature. Key findings:
- Single frames produce large effects that decay over time (Chong & Druckman 2010)
- Competing frames partially neutralize each other (Chong & Druckman 2007)
- Frame strength matters more than timing (Chong & Druckman 2007)
- Counterframes work differently on strong vs. weak attitudes (Chong & Druckman 2013)
- Source credibility moderates framing effects (Druckman 2001)
- Party cues amplify frame acceptance (Slothuus & de Vreese 2010)

**Gap:** This entire literature studies COMPETING (opposed) frames, not COMPLEMENTARY (stacked/reinforcing) frames. No one has systematically studied what happens when two reinforcing justifications are provided and one subsequently fails.

### 4. Argument Quantity and Quality (well-developed)
The ELM tradition has established clear findings:
- Under low involvement, more arguments = more persuasion regardless of quality (Petty & Cacioppo 1984)
- Under high involvement, quality > quantity (Petty & Cacioppo 1984)
- Adding weak arguments to strong ones REDUCES persuasion via averaging (Obermaier & Koch 2024; Weaver et al. 2012)
- Many arguments increase perceived expertise but also perceived persuasive intent, which cancel out (Bergman et al. 2025)
- Nondiagnostic information dilutes diagnostic information (Nisbett et al. 1981)

**Gap:** This literature addresses INITIAL persuasion -- the effect of argument bundles on first impressions. No one has tested what happens to the persuasive bundle AFTER formation, when one component is actively disconfirmed. The proposed project extends this from "bundling at formation" to "unbundling via disconfirmation."

### 5. Motivated Reasoning and Disconfirmation Bias (well-developed)
Strong tradition in political psychology:
- People counterargue incongruent information (Taber & Lodge 2006; Edwards & Smith 1996)
- Corrections sometimes fail; backfire effects documented but not robust (Nyhan & Reifler 2010; cf. Wood & Porter 2019)
- Motivated reasoning is real but constrained by justifiability (Kunda 1990)
- Analytic thinking can moderate motivated reasoning (Pennycook & Rand 2019)

**Gap:** This literature studies how people process EXTERNAL disconfirmation of their beliefs. It does not study how the STRUCTURE of the belief's justificatory basis affects vulnerability to disconfirmation.

### 6. Reason-Based Choice (moderately developed)
- People seek and construct articulable reasons to justify choices (Shafir, Simonson & Tversky 1993)
- When reasons are clear, people choose; when reasons conflict or are absent, they defer
- Reason-giving per se has limited effects on attitude properties (Blumenau 2025)

**Gap:** The reason-based choice framework predicts that people who articulated BOTH normative and practical reasons should be MORE disrupted when one is removed -- the removal creates a specific gap in their actively-held justificatory structure. This prediction has not been tested.

### 7. Dual Process Reasoning (well-developed)
- Two systems: associative (similarity-based) and rule-based (logical/symbolic) (Sloman 1996)
- Normative reasoning may engage the rule-based system; practical reasoning may engage associative processing
- The systems can generate conflicting outputs simultaneously

**Gap:** No one has tested whether normative and practical justifications are processed by different reasoning systems in the context of compound political arguments, and whether this explains differential vulnerability to disconfirmation.

### 8. Attitude Strength and Basis (moderately developed)
Important work on dimensions of attitude strength:
- Attitude strength is multidimensional (Pomerantz et al. 1995)
- Matched attacks are more effective than mismatched attacks (Fabrigar & Petty 1999)
- Moral conviction is a distinct dimension with unique resistance properties (Skitka 2010, 2021)
- Ambivalence x certainty interaction predicts attitude instability (Luttrell et al. 2016)
- Deontological reasoning is less susceptible to framing than consequentialist reasoning (Tanner et al. 2008)

**Gap:** The matching hypothesis and the moral conviction literature predict ASYMMETRIC vulnerability -- normative components should be harder to disconfirm than practical ones. But no one has tested this within a HYBRID argument where both types coexist and one is selectively removed.

### 9. War Support and Justification Types (moderately developed)
Several important studies:
- Expected success is the primary predictor of war support (Gelpi et al. 2009)
- Democracy/morality and threat both matter for military force support (Tomz & Weeks 2013)
- Moral values systematically predict foreign policy attitudes along harm/fairness vs. loyalty/authority dimensions (Kertzer et al. 2014)
- Public moral reasoning follows revisionist just war principles; ad bellum justice contaminates in bello judgments (Sagan & Valentino 2020)
- War justifications shift dynamically over conflict life cycle; justification failure triggers discursive recalibration (Alexandrescu 2025)
- Iraq War opinion decline followed WMD disconfirmation (observational evidence from Pew, Gallup)

**Gap:** The war support literature documents that justification types matter and that disconfirmation events (WMD failure, Abu Ghraib) shift opinion. But it has not EXPERIMENTALLY tested whether hybrid-justified wars are more or less durable than single-justification wars. The normative/practical distinction maps directly onto just war theory's jus ad bellum categories (just cause = normative; reasonable chance of success = consequentialist; Walzer 1977; McMahan 2009), giving the experimental design construct validity.

### 10. Negativity Bias and Loss Aversion in Attitudes (foundational, not applied to arguments)
Strong theoretical foundations:
- Bad is stronger than good across domains (Baumeister et al. 2001)
- Loss aversion ~2:1 in monetary domains (Kahneman & Tversky 1979)
- Negative information weighs more heavily in evaluations (information integration literature)

**Gap:** Loss aversion has been applied to beliefs-as-possessions (endowment effect for beliefs) but NOT to the internal structure of arguments. No one has tested whether losing a justification reduces support more than gaining an equivalent justification increases it. This is H3's contribution.

## What Has NOT Been Done (The Gap)

### The Core Gap
**No one has experimentally tested the structural durability of compound (hybrid) political arguments under partial disconfirmation.**

More specifically, no study has:

1. **Pitted belief perseverance against argument-structure fragility.** The perseverance tradition predicts compound arguments should be resilient (surviving justification provides alternative explanation); the averaging/dilution tradition predicts they should be fragile (compound evaluation was built on an inflated base). No experiment has set these predictions against each other.

2. **Compared hybrid vs. pure argument durability directly.** No experiment has randomly assigned respondents to pure-normative, pure-practical, and hybrid (normative+practical) argument conditions, then subjected each to disconfirmation of one component, and measured the differential effect on attitude persistence.

3. **Tested the "credibility cascade" mechanism.** No study has measured whether disconfirmation of one justification undermines perceived strength of the SURVIVING justification. The proposed mediator is novel.

4. **Applied gain/loss asymmetry to justifications within arguments.** No study applies loss aversion to the addition vs. removal of justifications within a compound argument.

5. **Demonstrated cross-domain generality of argument structure effects.** The proposed project tests across war, trade, climate, and potentially immigration -- no existing paper makes a domain-general claim about hybrid argument fragility.

### Why the Gap Exists

The gap sits at the intersection of four literatures that rarely talk to each other:
- **Belief perseverance** (cognitive psychology) -- studies persistence of single-basis beliefs after discrediting
- **Framing/persuasion** (political science) -- studies competing frames, not stacked ones
- **Argument quality/quantity** (social psychology) -- studies initial evaluation, not post-formation disconfirmation
- **Attitude basis and matching** (social psychology) -- studies what makes attitudes vulnerable to attack, but not the structural implications of having MULTIPLE bases

The proposed project bridges all four by asking: when an attitude rests on multiple justificatory bases of different types (normative + practical), does the structure itself create fragility or resilience under partial disconfirmation? This question requires integrating insights from all four traditions.

## Where This Paper Fits

The proposed paper fills this gap by:

1. **Introducing the concept of "argument structure durability"** -- the idea that the combination of justifications has emergent properties (fragility or resilience) that differ from individual justifications considered separately

2. **Explicitly testing competing predictions** from belief perseverance (resilience) vs. averaging/dilution (fragility) literatures -- pitting two well-established mechanisms against each other in a new structural context

3. **Proposing and testing the "credibility cascade" mechanism** -- measuring whether disconfirmation of one justification undermines perceived credibility of the surviving one. Constrained by Wood & Porter (2019): the cascade operates through rational inference, not backfire

4. **Applying loss aversion to argument structure** -- testing whether the gain/loss asymmetry from prospect theory extends to the addition and removal of justifications

5. **Demonstrating domain generality** -- testing across multiple policy domains (war, trade, climate) to establish a general property of political argument processing

6. **Grounding the war domain in just war theory** -- the normative/practical distinction maps onto jus ad bellum categories (Walzer 1977; McMahan 2009), with empirical validation that the public reasons along these lines (Sagan & Valentino 2020)
