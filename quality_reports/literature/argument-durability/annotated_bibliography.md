## Category 1: DIRECTLY RELATED (Proximity 4-5)

These papers address the closest questions to the proposed research -- argument structure effects on persuasion durability, multi-reason persuasion, and the consequences of adding or removing justifications.

---

### Obermaier & Koch (2024) -- "The paradox of argument strength: how weak arguments undermine the persuasive effects of strong arguments"
- **Journal:** Scientific Reports, 14, Article 22244
- **Summary:** Four experimental studies on political and health topics show that presenting weak supporting arguments alongside a strong argument REDUCES the persuasive effect of the strong argument (averaging pattern). Moderate-strength additions show an additive pattern. This is the most directly relevant finding: adding a weaker justification can undermine the overall case.
- **Identification strategy:** Between-subjects experiments varying argument strength combinations.
- **Key data source:** Online experiments on political and health topics.
- **Main result:** Weak + strong arguments = averaging (less persuasive than strong alone); moderate + strong = additive (more persuasive).
- **Proximity score:** 5 -- This is the CLOSEST existing paper. It shows that adding weaker arguments undermines stronger ones. However, it studies initial persuasion, NOT what happens when one argument is later disconfirmed. The argument-durability project extends this by asking: what happens to the hybrid argument AFTER formation, when one leg is actively disconfirmed?

### Bergman, Hussein, Catapano & Tormala (2025) -- "Fifteen Reasons You Should Read This Paper: How Providing Many Arguments Increases Perceptions of Both Expertise and Persuasive Intent"
- **Journal:** Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin (published online September 2025)
- **Summary:** Five experiments (N > 3,000) show that increasing the number of arguments creates COMPETING effects: more arguments increase perceived expertise (pro-persuasion) but also increase perceived persuasive intent (anti-persuasion). These suppress each other, yielding no net benefit. Nine arguments were no more persuasive than one.
- **Identification strategy:** Online experiments varying argument quantity.
- **Key data source:** Online experiments with 3,000+ participants.
- **Main result:** Argument quantity increases both perceived expertise and persuasive intent, which cancel out.
- **Proximity score:** 4 -- Directly relevant to argument quantity effects but focused on initial persuasion, not durability under disconfirmation.

### Weaver, Garcia & Schwarz (2012) -- "The Presenter's Paradox"
- **Journal:** Journal of Consumer Research, 39(3), 445-460
- **Summary:** Demonstrates that evaluators use an AVERAGING model rather than an ADDING model when assessing bundles. Adding a mildly favorable item to a highly favorable one reduces the overall evaluation, even though presenters expect it to help. Tested across scholarships, product bundles, and other contexts.
- **Identification strategy:** Series of experiments comparing presenter predictions to evaluator judgments.
- **Key data source:** Lab and online experiments on consumer judgments.
- **Main result:** Adding mildly positive items dilutes the value of bundles; evaluators average, presenters add.
- **Proximity score:** 4 -- The averaging mechanism is central to understanding why hybrid arguments might be fragile. If evaluators average normative and practical justifications, adding a weaker practical leg dilutes the normative one. However, this is about initial evaluation, not post-formation disconfirmation.

### Nisbett, Zukier & Lemley (1981) -- "The dilution effect: Nondiagnostic information weakens the implications of diagnostic information"
- **Journal:** Cognitive Psychology, 13(2), 248-277
- **Summary:** Adding nondiagnostic (irrelevant) information to diagnostic information weakens the extremity of judgments through a similarity-based mechanism. Subjects given mixed information made less extreme predictions than those given only diagnostic information.
- **Identification strategy:** Between-subjects experiments varying information composition.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments with undergraduates.
- **Main result:** Nondiagnostic information dilutes the impact of diagnostic information on predictions.
- **Proximity score:** 4 -- Establishes that adding information (even non-contradictory) can weaken judgments. The proposed project asks what happens when information is not merely nondiagnostic but actively disconfirmed -- a stronger version of dilution.

### Alexandrescu (2025) -- "The Wartime Justification Trajectory: A Dynamic Approach to Justifying Wars in the 21st Century"
- **Journal:** Politics and Governance, 13, DOI: 10.17645/pag.10044
- **Summary:** Proposes a dynamic four-phase framework (initial justification, conflict dynamics, social reactions, post-conflict evaluations) for how military intervention justifications evolve. Legitimacy is treated as a fluctuating condition shaped by political, social, strategic, and normative terrain. Case studies from Afghanistan (counterterrorism wore thin), Iraq (WMD justification collapsed), and Ukraine show how justification failure reshapes public support through discursive recalibration.
- **Identification strategy:** Qualitative case study analysis combining discourse analysis with public opinion data.
- **Key data source:** Afghanistan, Iraq, Ukraine rhetoric and polling data.
- **Main result:** War justifications are inherently dynamic; when stated justifications collapse, leaders engage in discursive recalibration and public support shifts.
- **Proximity score:** 4 -- Directly addresses justification failure in war contexts but is descriptive/qualitative, not experimental, and does not test hybrid vs. pure argument structure.

## Category 2: BELIEF PERSEVERANCE AND CONTINUED INFLUENCE (Proximity 3-5)

**This category was added in the second pass.** The belief perseverance literature is the most important competing/complementary tradition for the argument-durability project. Its core finding -- that beliefs PERSIST even after the evidential basis is removed -- stands in apparent tension with the "fragility" hypothesis. The distinction must be explicitly addressed.

---

### Ross, Lepper & Hubbard (1975) -- "Perseverance in self-perception and social perception: Biased attributional processes in the debriefing paradigm"
- **Journal:** Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 32(5), 880-892
- **Summary:** The founding paper of belief perseverance research. Subjects received false feedback about their performance on a novel discrimination task (success or failure), then were thoroughly debriefed that the feedback was fabricated. Despite complete debriefing, subjects continued to believe impressions formed from the false feedback. Both actors and observers showed substantial perseverance of initial impressions. A "process" debriefing (explaining the perseverance mechanism itself) was more effective at eliminating erroneous self-perceptions.
- **Identification strategy:** Between-subjects experiments with debriefing manipulation.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments with undergraduates.
- **Main result:** Beliefs formed from subsequently discredited evidence persist; standard outcome debriefing is insufficient to eliminate them.
- **Proximity score:** 4 -- **Critical competing finding.** If beliefs persist after evidential discrediting, then the "fragility" hypothesis (H1, H2) faces an empirical challenge: maybe hybrid arguments are NOT fragile because the surviving justification persists regardless of whether the other was discredited. The key distinction is that Ross et al. study beliefs with a SINGLE evidential basis (one piece of feedback), while the argument-durability project studies beliefs with COMPOUND justificatory structure (normative + practical). The perseverance effect may be strongest for simple beliefs and weaker for compound ones where the disconfirmation of one component makes the remaining component's insufficiency salient.

### Anderson, Lepper & Ross (1980) -- "Perseverance of social theories: The role of explanation in the persistence of discredited information"
- **Journal:** Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 39(6), 1037-1049
- **Summary:** Subjects received fictitious case studies suggesting either a positive or negative relationship between risk-taking and firefighter success. Some generated causal explanations; others did not. All were then thoroughly debriefed. Even after debriefing, subjects' theories persisted -- and this perseverance was amplified when subjects had generated causal explanations. The explanation process creates independent cognitive support structures that survive the removal of the original evidence.
- **Identification strategy:** Between-subjects experiment with explanation manipulation and debriefing.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments with undergraduates.
- **Main result:** Once people generate causal explanations for a theory, discrediting the evidence does not eliminate the theory. Explanation generation amplifies perseverance by ~20-30%.
- **Proximity score:** 5 -- **Most directly competing paper.** This is the strongest theoretical challenge to the fragility hypothesis. If generating explanations creates independent cognitive supports, then hybrid arguments -- where people have generated BOTH normative and practical reasons -- should be MORE resistant to partial disconfirmation, not less. The proposed project MUST explicitly address this: the Anderson et al. mechanism predicts resilience (the "possible surprise finding" in the research spec), while the averaging/dilution mechanism predicts fragility. The experimental design can distinguish these.

### Lord, Ross & Lepper (1979) -- "Biased assimilation and attitude polarization: The effects of prior theories on subsequently considered evidence"
- **Journal:** Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 37(11), 2098-2109
- **Summary:** Proponents and opponents of capital punishment were shown identical mixed evidence (one study supporting, one opposing deterrence). Both sides rated the study supporting their prior view as more convincing and reported that the mixed evidence STRENGTHENED their initial position. Exposure to the same balanced evidence produced INCREASED polarization.
- **Identification strategy:** Between-subjects experiment with strong-attitude holders exposed to mixed evidence.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiment with 48 undergraduates.
- **Main result:** Prior beliefs bias evaluation of subsequent evidence; mixed evidence increases polarization rather than moderating it.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Relevant for understanding how people process disconfirming information about political positions. The biased assimilation mechanism predicts that people will scrutinize the disconfirmation of their justifications and may reject it, potentially buffering the fragility effect. However, when the disconfirmation is factual (not ambiguous evidence), biased assimilation may be weaker.

### Johnson & Seifert (1994) -- "Sources of the continued influence of misinformation in memory: When misinformation in memory affects later inferences"
- **Journal:** Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition, 20(6), 1420-1436
- **Summary:** Established the "continued influence effect" (CIE): even after correction, participants continued to draw inferences based on discredited misinformation -- but only when it provided causal structure for the event narrative. Merely incidental misinformation did not show the effect. Providing a plausible causal alternative that replaces the causal structure mitigated the continued influence.
- **Identification strategy:** Experiments with narratives containing misinformation and corrections.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments measuring inference-making after correction.
- **Main result:** Discredited information continues to influence inferences when it provides causal structure; providing an alternative explanation mitigates the effect.
- **Proximity score:** 4 -- **Directly relevant to mechanism design.** The CIE predicts that if the practical justification for war provided causal structure ("we fight because it works"), then even after disconfirmation ("it's not working"), people may continue to draw inferences as if it were true. This is a form of RESILIENCE, not fragility. However, the CIE paradigm involves simple narratives with single pieces of misinformation, not compound argument structures. The argument-durability project asks whether the presence of a surviving alternative justification (normative) changes the CIE dynamic.

### Wood & Porter (2019) -- "The Elusive Backfire Effect: Mass Attitudes' Steadfast Factual Adherence"
- **Journal:** Political Behavior, 41(1), 135-163
- **Summary:** Five experiments (N > 10,100) tested 52 issues where backfire effects should be expected based on prior theory. Found NO corrections capable of triggering backfire. Citizens generally heed factual corrections, even on polarized issues. The original backfire effect (Nyhan & Reifler 2010) appears far more elusive than previously thought.
- **Identification strategy:** Large-scale between-subjects experiments with factual corrections across 52 issues.
- **Key data source:** Online experiments with 10,100+ US adults.
- **Main result:** Backfire effects are essentially non-existent across 52 issues; people generally update toward correct information.
- **Proximity score:** 4 -- **Constrains the credibility cascade mechanism.** If people generally accept corrections and update (no backfire), then the "credibility cascade" (H5) cannot rely on a backfire-like mechanism where disconfirmation makes things worse through reactance. Instead, the cascade must operate through a different channel: rational inference ("if they were wrong about X, maybe they're wrong about Y too") rather than motivated irrationality. This paper also suggests that disconfirmation in the proposed experiment WILL shift attitudes (corrections work), making the key question not WHETHER disconfirmation moves opinion but HOW MUCH MORE it moves opinion in hybrid vs. pure conditions.

### Lewandowsky, Ecker, Seifert, Schwarz & Cook (2012) -- "Misinformation and Its Correction: Continued Influence and Successful Debiasing"
- **Journal:** Psychological Science in the Public Interest, 13(3), 106-131
- **Summary:** Comprehensive review of misinformation persistence and correction. Key findings: (1) retractions rarely eliminate reliance on misinformation fully, (2) providing an alternative explanation is more effective than simple negation, (3) familiarity with misinformation can paradoxically increase belief, (4) source credibility and worldview consistency moderate correction effectiveness.
- **Identification strategy:** Review paper synthesizing experimental literature.
- **Key data source:** Published experimental research on misinformation.
- **Main result:** Misinformation continues to influence reasoning after correction; effective correction requires alternative explanations, not just negation.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Important for mechanism design. If disconfirmation of a practical justification is framed as "this reason is wrong" (simple negation), the continued influence effect predicts persistence. But in the hybrid argument context, the normative justification serves as a ready-made "alternative explanation" for maintaining the position, which Lewandowsky et al. predict should facilitate updating. This creates an interesting tension: the normative leg may help people ACCEPT the practical disconfirmation (facilitating clean updating) rather than resist it.

## Category 3: SAME METHOD, DIFFERENT CONTEXT (Methodological Precedent)

---

### Petty & Cacioppo (1984) -- "The effects of involvement on responses to argument quantity and quality"
- **Journal:** Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 46(1), 69-81
- **Summary:** Under low involvement, more arguments = more persuasion regardless of quality (peripheral route). Under high involvement, argument quality matters more than quantity (central route). Attitudes formed via central route are more persistent and resistant to counterpersuasion.
- **Identification strategy:** Between-subjects experiments varying involvement, quality, and quantity.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments with undergraduates.
- **Main result:** Quantity matters peripherally; quality matters centrally. Central-route attitudes are more durable.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Foundational for argument quantity effects, including the possibility that adding a weak practical leg (quantity increase) could backfire under high elaboration.

### Anderson (1971) -- "Integration Theory and Attitude Change"
- **Journal:** Psychological Review, 78(3), 171-206
- **Summary:** Applies a theory of information integration to attitudes and social judgments. Proposes that people integrate multiple pieces of information via weighted averaging rather than simple addition. Each informational stimulus is characterized by two parameters: scale value and weight. A highly desirable trait PLUS a moderately desirable one yields a LOWER impression than the highly desirable trait alone.
- **Identification strategy:** Series of impression formation experiments with algebraic modeling.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments on person perception and attitude formation.
- **Main result:** Weighted averaging, not addition, describes how people combine evaluative information.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Theoretical foundation for why hybrid arguments might be evaluated as weaker than pure strong arguments. The averaging model predicts that adding a moderate-strength justification (practical) to a strong one (normative) yields a weaker overall impression.

### Fabrigar & Petty (1999) -- "The role of the affective and cognitive bases of attitudes in susceptibility to affectively and cognitively based persuasion"
- **Journal:** Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin, 25(3), 363-381
- **Summary:** Persuasion is more effective when the type of appeal matches the attitude basis. Affect-based attitudes are more vulnerable to affective attacks; cognition-based attitudes are more vulnerable to cognitive attacks.
- **Identification strategy:** Experiments creating attitudes with different bases and attacking with matched/mismatched messages.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments with novel attitude objects.
- **Main result:** Matching attack type to attitude basis increases vulnerability to persuasion.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- The matching hypothesis is directly relevant: if a hybrid argument has both affective/normative and cognitive/practical bases, it may be vulnerable to BOTH types of attack, making it more fragile than a pure attitude.

### Coppock, Ekins & Kirby (2018) -- "The Long-lasting Effects of Newspaper Op-Eds on Public Opinion"
- **Journal:** Quarterly Journal of Political Science, 13(1), 59-87
- **Summary:** Op-eds shift policy attitudes by ~0.5 scale points on a 7-point scale, and effects persist for at least one month. Similar effects across party, ideology, elite vs. mass samples.
- **Identification strategy:** Randomized panel experiments with follow-up surveys.
- **Key data source:** MTurk and elite panel experiments.
- **Main result:** Persuasion effects from single-argument op-eds are substantial and persistent.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Relevant for understanding baseline attitude durability after persuasion, but single-argument, not multi-justification.

### Ahluwalia, Burnkrant & Unnava (2000) -- "Consumer Response to Negative Publicity: The Moderating Role of Commitment"
- **Journal:** Journal of Marketing Research, 37(2), 203-214
- **Summary:** Brand commitment moderates the negativity effect: highly committed consumers counterargue negative publicity and resist attitude change. Low-commitment consumers are more susceptible. Three studies demonstrate this moderation.
- **Identification strategy:** Experiments varying brand commitment and exposure to negative publicity.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments on consumer behavior.
- **Main result:** Commitment buffers against negative information through counterargument.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Relevant analogy: "commitment" to a policy position may moderate the fragility of hybrid arguments. The counterargument mechanism parallels motivated reasoning in political contexts.

### Voelkel & Feinberg (2018) -- "Morally Reframed Arguments Can Affect Support for Political Candidates"
- **Journal:** Social Psychological and Personality Science (published online 2017; PMC 2018)
- **Summary:** Arguments opposing a political candidate are more persuasive when framed in the target's moral values (e.g., loyalty for conservatives, fairness for liberals). Demonstrates that moral framing type matters for political persuasion.
- **Identification strategy:** Online experiments varying moral framing of anti-candidate messages.
- **Key data source:** Online experiments with US adults.
- **Main result:** Morally reframed arguments (matching target's values) are more persuasive than non-matching moral arguments.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Shows that the TYPE of moral/normative argument matters for persuasion effectiveness. Relevant for designing the normative component of hybrid arguments in the proposed experiment.

## Category 4: SAME CONTEXT, DIFFERENT METHOD (War/Foreign Policy Justifications)

---

### Tomz & Weeks (2013) -- "Public Opinion and the Democratic Peace"
- **Journal:** American Political Science Review, 107(4), 849-865
- **Summary:** Survey experiments show Americans and Britons are less supportive of military strikes against democracies vs. autocracies. The mechanism operates through perceptions of threat (practical) and morality (normative), not cost expectations.
- **Identification strategy:** Randomized survey experiments embedded in public opinion polls.
- **Key data source:** Original survey experiments in US and UK.
- **Main result:** Shared democracy reduces support for force via moral and threat perceptions.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Demonstrates that normative and practical considerations both matter for war support. Could be extended to ask: what happens when one of these is disconfirmed?

### Gelpi, Feaver & Reifler (2009) -- "Paying the Human Costs of War"
- **Journal:** Book, Princeton University Press
- **Summary:** Public tolerance for casualties depends on cost-benefit calculations: expected success is the strongest predictor, with policy objective (justification type) secondary. Public is not reflexively casualty-phobic but conditional on success expectations.
- **Identification strategy:** Observational analysis of polling data + survey experiments.
- **Key data source:** Gallup, Pew, original Iraq War surveys.
- **Main result:** Expected success > policy objective in predicting casualty tolerance.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Directly relevant to war-support domain. Casualties represent practical disconfirmation; the question becomes whether support drops more when both practical AND normative legs existed.

### Kertzer (2016) -- "Resolve in International Politics"
- **Journal:** Book, Princeton University Press
- **Summary:** Individual dispositions (time horizons, risk tolerance, honor, willpower) interact with situational factors to determine willingness to sustain costly commitments. Survey experiments show dispositional variation in resolve.
- **Identification strategy:** Survey experiments measuring dispositions and manipulating situations.
- **Key data source:** Original survey experiments.
- **Main result:** Resolve is a function of both dispositional and situational factors; they interact.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Studies persistence of support but through individual dispositions, not argument structure.

### Kertzer, Powers, Rathbun & Iyer (2014) -- "Moral Support: How Moral Values Shape Foreign Policy Attitudes"
- **Journal:** Journal of Politics, 76(3), 825-840
- **Summary:** Integrates Moral Foundations Theory with foreign policy attitudes using original survey data. The "individualizing" foundations (harm/care, fairness) predict cooperative internationalism; the "binding" foundations (authority, loyalty, purity) predict militant internationalism. Shows that the same moral values guiding daily life also structure foreign policy preferences.
- **Identification strategy:** Survey with moral foundations measures and foreign policy attitude batteries.
- **Key data source:** Nationally representative US survey.
- **Main result:** Moral foundations systematically predict foreign policy attitudes; different foundations map onto different policy orientations.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Directly relevant for understanding what KIND of normative reasoning underlies war support. If "binding" moral foundations drive militant attitudes, then the normative leg of a hybrid war argument may be grounded in loyalty/authority, which has different vulnerability properties than harm-based normative arguments.

### Sagan & Valentino (2018/2020) -- "Just War and Unjust Soldiers: American Public Opinion on the Moral Equality of Combatants"
- **Journal:** Ethics & International Affairs, 34(3), 283-308
- **Summary:** Survey experiment finding that the American public's moral reasoning about war is more consistent with revisionist just war theory (McMahan) than traditional just war theory (Walzer). Americans judged soldiers in unjust wars as less ethical even when battlefield conduct was identical, and a large proportion supported punishment for participation in unjust wars per se.
- **Identification strategy:** Survey experiment varying war justice and soldier conduct.
- **Key data source:** Original survey experiments with US adults.
- **Main result:** Public moral reasoning follows revisionist just war principles; war justice (ad bellum) contaminates judgments of soldier conduct (in bello).
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Demonstrates that the public applies moral reasoning to war evaluation in ways that track just war categories. The normative/practical distinction in the argument-durability project maps onto jus ad bellum dimensions: just cause (normative) and reasonable chance of success (consequentialist).

### Nyhan & Reifler (2010) -- "When Corrections Fail: The Persistence of Political Misperceptions"
- **Journal:** Political Behavior, 32(2), 303-330
- **Summary:** Corrections of political misperceptions sometimes fail to reduce belief in the misinformation and can produce a "backfire effect" where corrections increase misperceptions among ideologically motivated individuals. However, subsequent research has failed to reliably replicate the backfire effect.
- **Identification strategy:** Experiments with mock news articles containing corrections of political claims.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments with political stimuli.
- **Main result:** Corrections often fail; backfire effect documented but difficult to replicate.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Directly relevant to the mechanism: when one justification (e.g., WMDs in Iraq) is corrected/disconfirmed, does it backfire? The credibility cascade in the proposed project is a related but distinct phenomenon. Constrained by Wood & Porter (2019) finding that backfire is rare.

## Category 5: THEORETICAL FOUNDATIONS

---

### Taber & Lodge (2006) -- "Motivated Skepticism in the Evaluation of Political Beliefs"
- **Journal:** American Journal of Political Science, 50(3), 755-769
- **Summary:** Demonstrates motivated skepticism: people evaluate congruent arguments as stronger and incongruent ones as weaker. Both disconfirmation bias (counterarguing contrary arguments) and confirmation bias (seeking confirmatory evidence) lead to attitude polarization, especially among the most politically sophisticated.
- **Identification strategy:** Lab experiments on affirmative action and gun control.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments with convenience samples.
- **Main result:** Strong prior attitude effect; political sophistication amplifies bias; attitudes polarize after balanced exposure.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Foundational for motivated reasoning. The disconfirmation bias mechanism is central: when a practical justification is disconfirmed, motivated reasoning may either protect the surviving normative leg (buffering) or trigger a cascade of skepticism (fragility).

### Kunda (1990) -- "The Case for Motivated Reasoning"
- **Journal:** Psychological Bulletin, 108(3), 480-498
- **Summary:** People arrive at desired conclusions but are constrained by the need for seemingly reasonable justifications. Directional motivation biases the selection of cognitive strategies, while accuracy motivation enhances use of appropriate strategies.
- **Identification strategy:** Review paper.
- **Key data source:** Published experimental literature.
- **Main result:** Motivated reasoning is real but constrained by justifiability.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- If one justification fails, the justifiability constraint tightens: it becomes harder to maintain the position, potentially accelerating opinion change beyond what simple subtraction would predict.

### Chong & Druckman (2007) -- "A Theory of Framing and Opinion Formation in Competitive Elite Environments"
- **Journal:** Journal of Communication, 57(1), 99-118
- **Summary:** In competitive environments with multiple frames, frame effects depend on frame strength and timing. Strong frames counteract weak ones; when receiving balanced competing frames, people anchor on prior values.
- **Identification strategy:** Randomized experiments with strong/weak frame combinations.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments.
- **Main result:** Frame strength > frame timing in competitive settings.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Addresses how multiple frames interact, but focuses on COMPETING frames, not COMPLEMENTARY (stacked) ones. The proposed project's contribution is precisely this gap: what happens with stacked/reinforcing frames rather than competing ones?

### Chong & Druckman (2010) -- "Dynamic Public Opinion: Communication Effects over Time"
- **Journal:** American Political Science Review, 104(4), 663-680
- **Summary:** Framing effects decay over time. Competing messages received simultaneously neutralize each other, but when separated in time, recency dominates. Deliberate processors show greater persistence. Individual-level variation is high even when aggregate opinion appears stable.
- **Identification strategy:** Two panel experiments measuring opinion change over time.
- **Key data source:** Original panel experiments.
- **Main result:** Framing effects are temporally dynamic; recency dominates for most; deliberate processors resist decay.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Relevant method for studying durability. The temporal dynamics of hybrid argument effects (how long does the fragility/resilience persist?) could be studied using this panel design.

### Chong & Druckman (2013) -- "Counterframing Effects"
- **Journal:** Journal of Politics, 75(1), 1-16
- **Summary:** Tests how timing and repetition of counterframes affect their success. Effective counterframing depends on audience opinion strength: tactics that work on those with weak opinions may backfire on those with strong opinions.
- **Identification strategy:** Experiments varying counterframe timing and repetition.
- **Key data source:** Experiments using US Patriot Act framing.
- **Main result:** Counterframing effectiveness is contingent on audience attitude strength.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Relevant for understanding how disconfirmation (a form of counterframing) interacts with attitude strength. Prior attitude strength likely moderates the fragility of hybrid arguments.

### Druckman (2001) -- "On the Limits of Framing Effects: Who Can Frame?"
- **Journal:** Journal of Politics, 63(4), 1041-1066
- **Summary:** Framing effects depend on source credibility. Only credible sources produce framing effects.
- **Identification strategy:** Experiments manipulating frame content and source credibility.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments.
- **Main result:** Source credibility moderates framing effects.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Background on framing. If the source of a hybrid argument loses credibility when one leg fails, this could be the mechanism for credibility cascade.

### Druckman (2022) -- "A Framework for the Study of Persuasion"
- **Journal:** Annual Review of Political Science, 25, 65-88
- **Summary:** Comprehensive review organizing the fragmented persuasion literature into the Generalizing Persuasion (GP) Framework: actors, treatments, outcomes, settings. Notes the field lacks generalizable findings.
- **Identification strategy:** Review paper.
- **Key data source:** Published persuasion research.
- **Main result:** The persuasion literature is fragmented; the GP Framework organizes it.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Important for positioning. The argument-durability project contributes to the "treatments" dimension (specifically, treatment structure) of this framework.

### Kahneman & Tversky (1979) -- "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk"
- **Journal:** Econometrica, 47(2), 263-291
- **Summary:** Foundational paper on prospect theory. Losses loom larger than equivalent gains (~2:1 ratio). People evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point.
- **Identification strategy:** Choice experiments demonstrating deviations from expected utility.
- **Key data source:** Hypothetical choice experiments.
- **Main result:** Loss aversion: losses weighted approximately twice as much as equivalent gains.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Theoretical anchor for H3 (gain/loss asymmetry in argument structure).

### Baumeister, Bratslavsky, Finkenauer & Vohs (2001) -- "Bad Is Stronger Than Good"
- **Journal:** Review of General Psychology, 5(4), 323-370
- **Summary:** Comprehensive review: negative events, emotions, feedback, and information have more impact than positive equivalents across domains. Bad impressions form faster and resist disconfirmation more.
- **Identification strategy:** Review paper (10,000+ citations).
- **Key data source:** Published research across many domains.
- **Main result:** Negativity bias is pervasive.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Additional theoretical support for H3: losing a justification (bad) should hurt more than gaining one (good) helps.

### Skitka (2010, 2021) -- "The Psychology of Moral Conviction"
- **Journal:** Social and Personality Psychology Compass (2010); Annual Review of Psychology (2021)
- **Summary:** Moral convictions are psychologically distinct from other strong attitudes: more resistant to authority/peer influence, associated with intolerance for dissent, greater political engagement. They are perceived as universal and objective truths.
- **Identification strategy:** Multiple studies across samples.
- **Key data source:** Multiple datasets.
- **Main result:** Moral conviction is a distinct dimension of attitude strength predicting resistance to change.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Highly relevant for predicting that normative (morally-grounded) legs of hybrid arguments should be harder to disconfirm than practical legs.

### Tanner, Medin & Iliev (2008) -- "Influence of deontological versus consequentialist orientations on act choices and framing effects"
- **Journal:** European Journal of Social Psychology, 38(5), 757-769
- **Summary:** Deontological orientation and protected values are associated with reduced susceptibility to framing effects. Consequentialist orientation predicts greater framing susceptibility.
- **Identification strategy:** Experiments measuring moral orientation and manipulating frames.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments.
- **Main result:** Deontological reasoning = less frameable; consequentialist reasoning = more frameable.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- If deontological/normative reasoning is more robust to framing, it may also be more robust to disconfirmation, predicting asymmetric fragility in hybrid arguments.

### Sloman (1996) -- "The Empirical Case for Two Systems of Reasoning"
- **Journal:** Psychological Bulletin, 119(1), 3-22
- **Summary:** Argues for two distinct reasoning systems: an associative system reflecting similarity and contiguity, and a rule-based system operating on symbolic structures with logical content. Both systems can simultaneously generate different responses to the same problem. The rule-based system can suppress (but not eliminate) the associative system.
- **Identification strategy:** Review paper synthesizing evidence from multiple reasoning domains.
- **Key data source:** Published reasoning research across multiple paradigms.
- **Main result:** Two systems of reasoning operate in parallel, sometimes producing conflicting outputs; the rule-based system can override but not eliminate associative processing.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- **Relevant for the normative/practical distinction.** Normative (deontological) reasoning may engage the rule-based system ("it is wrong to abandon allies"), while practical (consequentialist) reasoning may engage associative processing ("costs exceed benefits"). If the two systems process hybrid arguments through different channels, disconfirmation of the practical component (challenging associative cost-benefit calculations) may not propagate to the rule-based normative component. This predicts asymmetric fragility and potentially explains why normative legs might be more durable.

### Shafir, Simonson & Tversky (1993) -- "Reason-based choice"
- **Journal:** Cognition, 49(1-2), 11-36
- **Summary:** People seek and construct reasons to justify choices. The availability of articulable reasons drives choice behavior. When reasons are clear and compelling, people choose; when reasons are absent or conflicting, they defer or reverse.
- **Identification strategy:** Experimental studies on consumer and hypothetical choices.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments.
- **Main result:** Clear, articulable reasons drive choice.
- **Proximity score:** 4 (upgraded from 3) -- **The reason-based choice framework generates a specific prediction for hybrid arguments that the critic correctly identified as underplayed.** If people anchor on articulable reasons, then having TWO articulable reasons (normative + practical) should make the initial position more robust -- but LOSING one reason is particularly destabilizing because it removes a justificatory anchor that was actively being used. People who provided both normative and practical reasons for their position should be MORE disrupted when one reason is removed than people who only had one reason, because the removal creates a specific gap in their justificatory structure that they explicitly held. This directly supports the fragility hypothesis (H1, H2) and provides a mechanism distinct from both averaging and credibility cascade.

### Zaller (1992) -- "The Nature and Origins of Mass Opinion"
- **Journal:** Book, Cambridge University Press
- **Summary:** The RAS model: opinions are constructed on the fly from accessible considerations. More aware citizens are more selective; less aware citizens accept more messages uncritically.
- **Identification strategy:** Theoretical model tested against survey data.
- **Key data source:** National surveys (NES).
- **Main result:** Opinions are constructed from accessible considerations; elite messages shape the raw material.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- If one justification is disconfirmed, it may become an accessible NEGATIVE consideration that poisons the entire evaluation, especially for high-awareness individuals who integrated it deeply.

### Haidt (2001) -- "The Emotional Dog and Its Rational Tail"
- **Journal:** Psychological Review, 108(4), 814-834
- **Summary:** Social intuitionist model: moral judgments are driven by quick intuitions; moral reasoning is post-hoc rationalization. The "rational tail" follows the "emotional dog," not the reverse.
- **Identification strategy:** Review/theoretical paper.
- **Key data source:** Published moral psychology research.
- **Main result:** Intuitions drive moral judgment; reasoning is post-hoc justification.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- If normative arguments engage intuitions rather than reasoning, they may be more robust to disconfirmation (intuitions persist even when reasons fail). This predicts asymmetry between normative and practical leg failure.

### Edwards & Smith (1996) -- "A disconfirmation bias in the evaluation of arguments"
- **Journal:** Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 71(1), 5-24
- **Summary:** People scrutinize belief-inconsistent arguments more, generate more counterarguments, and judge them as weaker. Emotional conviction amplifies disconfirmation bias.
- **Identification strategy:** Experiments with pro/con arguments on controversial topics.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments.
- **Main result:** Disconfirmation bias: contrary arguments are scrutinized more and judged weaker.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- The disconfirmation bias mechanism explains why people might resist disconfirmation of their justifications, but not what happens to the structural integrity of multi-justification positions when one justification falls.

## Category 6: JUST WAR THEORY (Normative Framework for War Domain)

**Added in second pass.** The project uses war as a primary domain, and the normative/practical split maps directly onto just war theory categories.

---

### Walzer (1977/2015) -- *Just and Unjust Wars: A Moral Argument with Historical Illustrations*
- **Publisher:** Basic Books (5th edition 2015)
- **Summary:** The foundational modern work on just war theory. Walzer articulates the moral framework governing the resort to war (jus ad bellum) and conduct in war (jus in bello). Key jus ad bellum criteria include: just cause, right intention, legitimate authority, proportionality, reasonable chance of success, and last resort. Walzer defends the "moral equality of combatants" (soldiers on both sides have equal moral standing) and the separation of ad bellum from in bello judgments.
- **Key relevance:** The normative/practical distinction in the argument-durability project maps directly onto Walzer's jus ad bellum categories. "Just cause" and "right intention" are normative justifications; "reasonable chance of success" and "proportionality" are consequentialist/practical ones. When elites justify war on both normative AND practical grounds, they are implicitly invoking multiple jus ad bellum criteria -- making the project's experimental manipulation a test of how these criteria interact under stress.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Provides the normative framework that structures how war justifications are categorized as normative vs. practical. Essential background for the war domain scenarios.

### McMahan (2009) -- *Killing in War*
- **Publisher:** Oxford University Press (Uehiro Series in Practical Ethics)
- **Summary:** Challenges traditional just war theory's moral equality of combatants. Argues that soldiers bear moral responsibility for the wars they choose to fight, not just their conduct. Soldiers fighting for an unjust cause act impermissibly even if they follow the laws of war. This "revisionist" view ties the morality of fighting to the justice of the cause (ad bellum), breaking the traditional separation of ad bellum and in bello.
- **Key relevance:** McMahan's revisionism predicts that the public should be sensitive to the JUSTICE of the cause (normative dimension). If the normative justification for war is disconfirmed ("the cause was not just"), McMahan's framework predicts a stronger effect than if only the practical justification fails ("it's not working"). This aligns with the predicted asymmetry between normative and practical disconfirmation. Sagan & Valentino (2018/2020) found empirically that the American public's reasoning is more revisionist than traditionalist.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Philosophical background for the war domain that helps predict asymmetric effects of normative vs. practical disconfirmation.

## Category 7: METHODS PAPERS

---

### McGuire (1961) -- Inoculation Theory
- **Summary:** Attitudes can be made resistant to persuasion by pre-exposure to weakened counterarguments. Two components: threat recognition and refutational preemption.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Could inform experimental design: does forewarning that one justification might fail buffer the fragility effect?

### Sniderman & Theriault (2004) -- "The Structure of Political Argument and the Logic of Issue Framing"
- **Journal:** Chapter in Saris & Sniderman (eds.), Studies in Public Opinion, Princeton University Press
- **Summary:** Examines counterframing strategies in competitive argument environments. Uncontested single frames cause more opinion deviation than balanced competing frames.
- **Identification strategy:** Survey experiments varying frame exposure.
- **Key data source:** Original survey experiments.
- **Main result:** Competing balanced frames reduce framing effects; individuals anchor on prior values with balanced information.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Studies argument structure but in competitive (opposed) rather than complementary (stacked) settings.

### Slothuus & de Vreese (2010) -- "Political Parties, Motivated Reasoning, and Issue Framing Effects"
- **Journal:** Journal of Politics, 72(3), 630-645
- **Summary:** Party cues interact with framing: citizens follow frames from their own party more. Effect is stronger on central issues and among the politically aware.
- **Identification strategy:** Two experiments in nationally representative Danish survey.
- **Key data source:** Danish survey experiments.
- **Main result:** Partisan motivation amplifies frame acceptance.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Party cues likely moderate hybrid argument fragility: co-partisan sources may buffer against disconfirmation.

### Pomerantz, Chaiken & Tordesillas (1995) -- "Attitude strength and resistance processes"
- **Journal:** Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 69(3), 408-419
- **Summary:** Attitude strength is multidimensional: Commitment (selective elaboration, polarization) and Embeddedness (information seeking, selective memory) produce different resistance processes.
- **Identification strategy:** Experiments measuring multiple attitude strength indicators.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments.
- **Main result:** Different dimensions of attitude strength produce different resistance mechanisms.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Background on attitude strength dimensions relevant to measuring why some attitudes resist disconfirmation.

### Luttrell, Petty & Brinol (2016) -- "Ambivalence and certainty can interact to predict attitude stability over time"
- **Journal:** Journal of Experimental Social Psychology
- **Summary:** Ambivalence x certainty interaction: when ambivalence is high, higher certainty actually predicts LESS stability (counterintuitive). Low ambivalence + high certainty = most stable.
- **Identification strategy:** Longitudinal experiments measuring stability over time.
- **Key data source:** Lab experiments.
- **Main result:** High ambivalence + high certainty = less stable attitudes.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Hybrid arguments may induce ambivalence (mixed normative/practical bases), which could interact with certainty to produce fragility.

### Blumenau (2024/2025) -- "Does Reason-Giving Affect Political Attitudes?"
- **Journal:** British Journal of Political Science
- **Summary:** Survey experiment asking respondents to provide reasons for their political positions. Found very limited effects of reason-giving on constraint, stability, or polarization of attitudes.
- **Identification strategy:** Survey experiment with reason-giving manipulation.
- **Key data source:** UK political attitudes survey.
- **Main result:** Reason-giving has minimal effects on attitude properties.
- **Proximity score:** 3 -- Relevant contrast: this paper asks whether generating reasons changes attitudes. The proposed project asks whether removing a reason changes attitudes -- a different and potentially more consequential manipulation.

### Pennycook & Rand (2019) -- "Lazy, not biased: Susceptibility to partisan fake news is better explained by lack of reasoning than by motivated reasoning"
- **Journal:** Cognition, 188, 39-50
- **Summary:** Political misinformation susceptibility is better explained by lack of analytic thinking than by motivated partisan reasoning. More reflective thinkers are better at discerning fake from real news regardless of partisan alignment.
- **Identification strategy:** Survey experiments with CRT and fake news accuracy judgments.
- **Key data source:** Online experiments with US adults.
- **Main result:** Cognitive laziness > motivated reasoning in explaining misinformation susceptibility.
- **Proximity score:** 2 -- Relevant for understanding individual-level moderators: analytic thinkers may process disconfirmation of hybrid arguments differently than intuitive thinkers.

## Papers Searched For But NOT Found

The following concepts were searched for extensively but yielded no direct matches, suggesting a literature gap:

1. **No paper directly tests what happens when one leg of a multi-justification political argument is actively disconfirmed.** The closest papers (Obermaier & Koch 2024, Weaver et al. 2012) study initial evaluation, not post-formation disconfirmation. The belief perseverance tradition (Ross et al. 1975; Anderson et al. 1980) studies persistence after discrediting but with SINGLE evidential bases, not compound argument structures.

2. **No paper explicitly studies "hybrid argument fragility"** as a concept. The term does not appear in the literature.

3. **No paper experimentally tests "credibility cascade"** -- where disconfirmation of one justification undermines perceived credibility of the surviving justification. The continued influence effect (Johnson & Seifert 1994) is related but studies whether discredited information continues to influence reasoning, not whether it undermines OTHER information. This is the core mechanism (H5) and appears to be genuinely novel.

4. **No paper applies loss aversion specifically to argument structure** (H3). Loss aversion has been applied to beliefs-as-possessions (endowment effect for beliefs) but not to the gain/loss of individual justifications within a compound argument.

5. **No paper systematically compares the durability of hybrid vs. pure normative vs. pure practical political arguments** under disconfirmation. This is the core design and appears to be novel.

6. **No empirical paper directly tests how just war criteria (normative vs. consequentialist) interact in public opinion when one is disconfirmed.** Sagan & Valentino (2018/2020) test moral equality of combatants, and Kertzer et al. (2014) test moral foundations and foreign policy, but neither examines the structural interaction of normative and practical war justifications under partial disconfirmation.
